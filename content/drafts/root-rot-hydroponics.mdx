If your hydroponic roots are brown, slimy, and smell like a swamp, you have root rot — and if any white root mass is still visible, you can usually save the plant. Trim the dead tissue with sterile scissors, rinse the survivors in diluted 3% hydrogen peroxide (3 ml per litre), drain and wipe the reservoir, refill with fresh nutrients dosed at 3 ml of 3% H2O2 per gallon, then add Botanicare Hydroguard at 2 ml per gallon 24–48 hours later. That sequence is the standard hydroponic root rot treatment, and it works on every system from DWC to NFT.

The cause is almost always Pythium — a water mould, not a true fungus. [Pythium aphanidermatum and Pythium dissotocum are the two species most often found on hydroponic basil, lettuce, and spinach](https://e-gro.org/pdf/E301.pdf), and [diagnosticians regularly find them co-infecting roots alongside Phytophthora, Fusarium, or Rhizoctonia](https://www.umass.edu/agriculture-food-environment/greenhouse-floriculture/fact-sheets/root-diseases-of-greenhouse-crops). That distinction matters: copper sprays and most generic "antifungal" garden products do not reliably kill oomycetes, which is why so many growers treat root rot and watch it return.

## What is hydroponic root rot?

Hydroponic root rot is a root infection driven mainly by Pythium species — oomycetes (water moulds), not true fungi — sometimes joined by Phytophthora, Fusarium, or Rhizoctonia. Because Pythium belongs to the kingdom Stramenopila rather than the fungi, copper-based and most generic antifungal garden products do not reliably kill it. That single misclassification is the most common reason root rot returns after treatment.

[Pythium aphanidermatum becomes a pathogen above 77°F (25°C)](https://ipm.ucanr.edu/agriculture/floriculture-and-ornamental-nurseries/pythium-root-rot/) per UC IPM, with a growth optimum reported between 95–104°F (35–40°C). Its cooler-loving cousin Pythium dissotocum dominates below that. Both exist at low background levels in tap and well water and only flip pathogenic when the reservoir warms, deoxygenates, or gets light leaks. [In recirculating systems, motile zoospores swim freely from an infected plant to every other root mass through the shared irrigation water](https://www.umass.edu/agriculture-food-environment/greenhouse-floriculture/fact-sheets/root-diseases-of-greenhouse-crops), which is why one infected net pot can take down an NFT loop in 48 hours.

This is the structural reason DWC and recirculating NFT systems have the worst reputation for root rot. Drain-to-waste, the [Kratky method](/blog/diy-hydroponic-garden), and media-based passive setups are inherently lower risk because plants are not sharing water.

## How to identify root rot

Root inspection is the only reliable early detection. By the time leaves yellow or wilt, the infection is well established — so lift the net pot every time you adjust nutrients or check EC. Healthy roots are white or cream, firm, and covered in fine fuzzy root hairs with no smell.

| Stage | Root colour | Texture | Smell |
|---|---|---|---|
| Early | Light brown root tips | Slightly slimy at tips only | Faint, slightly off |
| Moderate | Brown throughout, grey patches | Slimy, limp | Noticeable foul smell |
| Advanced | Dark brown to black | Collapsing when touched | Strong sulphurous odour |

Above-water signs — yellowing leaves that ignore nutrient changes, wilting despite a full reservoir, sudden stunted growth — are late-stage indicators. If you only catch root rot from the leaves, expect a slower recovery and a real chance the plant is past saving.

## The four conditions that cause hydroponic root rot

**1. Warm water.** Temperature is the dominant driver. [Colorado State recommends 68–72°F (20–22°C) at the root zone for preventing root diseases](https://planttalk.colostate.edu/topics/miscellaneous/2043-root-disease-problems-management-hydroponic-systems/), and warm-loving Pythium becomes aggressive once you cross 77°F. Warmth is a double hit — it accelerates Pythium reproduction and reduces how much oxygen the water can hold. At 68°F water holds about 9.1 mg/L dissolved oxygen at saturation; at 86°F that drops to about 7.6 mg/L.

**2. Low dissolved oxygen.** Roots respire aerobically, and so do the beneficial microbes that compete with Pythium. [Cornell CEA recommends maintaining greater than 6 ppm dissolved oxygen, ideally near saturation at 8–9 ppm](https://e-gro.org/pdf/E301.pdf) in the nutrient solution. Below 6 ppm, root cell membranes weaken and Pythium gets its entry point. For DWC, this means an air pump sized for at least 2× your reservoir volume, running 24/7 with a fine-bubble air stone. For Kratky, the air gap between the water surface and net pot base provides passive oxygenation — overfilling the reservoir removes it and is one of the most common beginner mistakes.

**3. Stagnant water.** Quiet reservoir corners let zoospores settle on roots. Active movement from an air pump or recirculating loop keeps spores suspended.

**4. Light exposure.** Any light reaching the reservoir drives algae. Algae competes with roots for dissolved oxygen, then dies back and leaves organic matter that Pythium feeds on. Every reservoir surface must be fully opaque — black containers, covered lids, sealed net-pot holes. A green tint means algae already won round one.

A related risk factor: high pH causes iron lockout, the plant gets nutrient-stressed, and stressed root tissue is easier for Pythium to colonise. See our [pH for hydroponics guide](/blog/ph-for-hydroponics) for the dosing math on keeping the reservoir at 5.5–6.5.

## Hydroponic root rot treatment, step by step

Treat root rot the day you identify it. Pythium spreads through shared reservoir water faster than most growers expect.

You need sterile scissors wiped with isopropyl alcohol, a bottle of 3% drugstore hydrogen peroxide, a clean rinse bucket, fresh nutrient solution, and Botanicare Hydroguard for the post-treatment recolonisation.

**Step 1: Remove the plant.** Lift the plant and net pot over a clean bucket, not back into the infected reservoir.

**Step 2: Trim infected roots.** Cut away all brown, grey, or black tissue back to healthy white roots. If most of the root mass is gone and the leaves have already collapsed, removal is the practical call — it stops the plant from reinfecting the next crop.

**Step 3: Rinse remaining roots in H2O2.** Mix 3 ml of 3% hydrogen peroxide per litre of clean water and submerge the surviving roots for 5 minutes. This dose — roughly 90 ppm — is more concentrated than the reservoir dose in Step 5 because direct contact with infected tissue needs the extra strength. If you only have 35% food-grade H2O2, the equivalent is 0.26 ml per litre. Do not let roots air-dry between steps.

**Step 4: Drain and wipe the reservoir.** Empty all water. Wipe every internal surface with a cloth dampened in the same diluted H2O2. Pythium spores survive on dry reservoir walls — skip this step and you reinfect the new water on day one.

**Step 5: Refill with fresh nutrients + low-dose H2O2.** Mix a complete nutrient batch at your normal EC and pH. Add 3 ml of 3% H2O2 per gallon (about 25 ppm) as a final reservoir sweep. The peroxide breaks down to water and oxygen within 24–48 hours, so it will not poison the system long-term.

**Step 6: Add Hydroguard after 24–48 hours.** Once the H2O2 has broken down, dose Botanicare Hydroguard at 2 ml per gallon. The Bacillus amyloliquefaciens colonises the clean root zone and competes with any surviving Pythium for space and food. Dosing it at the same time as H2O2 kills the bacteria before they can establish — the gap is non-negotiable.

## Prevention: four levers that eliminate the risk

Prevention is much easier than treatment. These four measures address the four root causes directly.

**Keep reservoir water below 72°F.** Highest-leverage step. Move the reservoir away from heat sources, wrap it in reflective insulation in warm rooms, and run an oversized air pump. The cheapest active cooling is a frozen 1-litre water bottle floated in the reservoir — one bottle drops a 5-gallon DWC by ~5–8°F for 2–3 hours. For chronic heat, an aquarium-grade 1/10 HP chiller is the permanent fix at ~$200–$400, with full hydroponic chillers (Active Aqua, Ecoplus) at ~$400–$700.

**Hit 6+ ppm dissolved oxygen.** Run a fine-bubble air pump rated for at least 2× your reservoir volume — a 10-gal-rated pump for a 5-gallon bucket. Fine bubbles transfer more oxygen per second than large ones because of the surface-area-to-volume ratio. In Kratky, leave the air gap intact and never overfill.

**Block every photon from the reservoir.** Use black or wrapped containers, sealed lids, and net-pot collars. Even pinhole light leaks support algae over time. A green water tint means you already lost round one — drain, clean, and reseal.

**Dose Hydroguard from day one.** Establishing the Bacillus amyloliquefaciens colony from the first fill is more effective than reacting to an outbreak. A quart bottle at 2 ml/gal lasts several grows for a home DWC.

### Does a UV sterilizer help?

UV sterilizers reduce Pythium populations in the bulk reservoir water, but [research on hydroponic tomato found UV treatment did not produce a corresponding reduction in root rot or yield loss](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1023/A:1008798710325). Zoospores released inside the root zone never pass through the lamp before reinfecting neighbouring roots, and UV degrades chelated iron and kills your beneficial Bacillus. For a home grower, the $150–$300 a UV unit costs is better spent on a chiller and consistent Hydroguard dosing.

### When to throw the crop out

If 60–70%+ of the root mass has collapsed, the leaves have wilted to structural failure, or the same plant has reinfected after two treatment cycles, compost it and sanitise the system. The downtime exceeds the value of one rescued lettuce.

## My take as a curator

Root rot is almost entirely preventable, and growers who keep getting it are almost always fighting the same two unfixed problems: reservoir temperature they have not measured, and light leaks they have not sealed. Address those two and Hydroguard handles the rest.

The hydrogen peroxide treatment gives you a clean slate — it kills both Pythium and your beneficial colony, which is why the 24–48 hour gap before reintroducing Hydroguard matters. What surprised me in the research: the temperature threshold is lower than most growers expect. 72°F feels cool, but it is right at the edge where Pythium starts winning. Reservoirs sitting at 75–78°F in a warm apartment are not running a slightly elevated risk — they are operating on an exponential curve. A $10 aquarium thermometer floated in the reservoir, checked weekly, is the simplest diagnostic tool most home growers skip.
