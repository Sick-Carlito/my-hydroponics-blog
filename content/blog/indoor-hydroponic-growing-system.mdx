One of the most common questions from people starting hydroponics is: *which system should I actually use?*

It's a fair question. There are at least five distinct methods, each with its own trade-offs on cost, complexity, and what crops it handles best. Most beginner guides pick one and say it's the best. That's not particularly useful if you have a different setup, budget, or set of goals.

Here's a straightforward comparison of the four systems you'll actually encounter as an indoor home grower.

## The Four Main Indoor Hydroponic Systems

### 1. Kratky Method — No Pump, No Electricity

The Kratky method is passive hydroponics. Plants sit in net pots above a reservoir of nutrient solution. As the plant draws down the solution, an air gap forms above the water, exposing roots to oxygen without any pump required.

**What makes it work:** The air gap is self-regulating. As roots grow longer, they chase the receding water level naturally.

**Best for:** Lettuce, herbs, spinach, and any leafy green you want to grow with minimal infrastructure.

**What it costs:** $20–$40 for a complete setup. Mason jars, a 5-gallon bucket, or any opaque container works. Net pots, a starter nutrient solution, and pH test strips round out the kit.

**The honest limitation:** Kratky doesn't scale well for high-volume growing, and the stagnant reservoir needs monitoring for algae if light gets in. Keep containers opaque.

---

### 2. Deep Water Culture (DWC) — The Best Starting Point for Most Growers

DWC suspends plant roots in an oxygenated nutrient solution inside a sealed reservoir. An air pump connected to an air stone keeps the water oxygenated so roots don't drown.

This is the system I'd recommend for most beginners because it's well-documented, inexpensive, and produces reliable results.

**What it costs:** $40–$70 for a single-bucket setup including:
- 5-gallon bucket: $6–$10
- Net pots (2-inch): $5–$8
- Air pump + air stone + tubing: $12–$18
- Growing medium (clay pebbles): $8–$12
- Nutrients: $12–$18
- pH test strips: $7

**Best for:** Lettuce (harvest in 4–6 weeks), basil, mint, kale, cilantro.

**The trade-off:** DWC uses more water than passive systems, and if the air pump fails, roots can suffocate within hours. It's worth having a spare pump on hand.

---

### 3. NFT (Nutrient Film Technique) — Best for Volume

NFT systems run a thin film of nutrient solution continuously through sloped channels. Plant roots sit partially in the channel and partially in air above it. Unlike DWC, NFT doesn't submerge roots — they absorb solution from the passing film and oxygen from the air gap above.

**What it costs:** DIY NFT channels run $50–$100 using PVC gutters or purpose-built channels. Commercial NFT setups range from $150 to $500+.

**Best for:** High-volume leafy green production. NFT is the method behind most commercial hydroponic lettuce operations. It scales easily by adding more channels.

**The trade-off:** NFT systems require the pump to run 24/7. A power outage or pump failure dries out roots quickly because there's no reservoir buffer like DWC. NFT also requires a slightly higher level of setup precision to ensure consistent slope and flow rate.

If you're growing more than 10–15 plants and have some DIY experience, NFT is worth the extra setup effort. For a first system, DWC is easier to troubleshoot.

---

### 4. Aeroponic Systems — Fastest Growth, Most Maintenance

Aeroponic systems mist plant roots with nutrient solution at timed intervals rather than submerging or filming them. Roots spend most of their time in air, maximizing oxygen exposure and accelerating growth.

Research backs the speed claim: aeroponic systems grow lettuce in **21–25 days** compared to **28–35 days** for NFT. The Tower Garden HOME and similar premium systems use this method.

**What it costs:** Commercial aeroponic systems start at ~$230 (AeroGarden Bounty) and run to $1,020+ (Tower Garden HOME). DIY aeroponic builds are possible but significantly more complex than DIY DWC or NFT.

**Best for:** Home growers who want maximum output, are comfortable with more maintenance, and have the budget for a quality system.

**The trade-off:** More components = more failure points. Misting nozzles clog. Timers fail. If roots dry out (which happens within minutes in some designs), plants suffer quickly. Aeroponic systems are the least forgiving for beginners and the most rewarding once you know what you're doing.

## What All Indoor Systems Have in Common

Regardless of which system you pick, these three variables apply to all of them:

**pH management:** The optimal range for most leafy greens is 5.5–6.5. Outside this range, plants can't absorb nutrients even if your solution is mixed correctly. Test pH before every reservoir refill and adjust as needed. pH test strips cost $7 for a pack that lasts months.

**Lighting:** All indoor systems need artificial lighting. Windows almost never provide enough light intensity or duration. Plan for 14–16 hours of full-spectrum LED light per day. A basic 9-watt grow light handles herbs and small greens; scale up to 45–100 watts for larger systems.

**Nutrient solution changes:** University of Minnesota Extension recommends changing your full nutrient solution every 6–7 weeks for short-season crops. Top off with plain water between changes.

## Which System Should You Start With?

- **Complete beginner, lowest budget:** Kratky method in a mason jar or 5-gallon bucket. Grow lettuce. Cost: $20–$40.
- **Beginner who wants to learn the mechanics:** DWC single bucket. More to manage but better preparation for scaling up. Cost: $40–$70.
- **Intermediate grower, 10+ plants:** DIY NFT channel setup. Produces volume efficiently. Cost: $80–$150.
- **Someone who wants plug-and-grow with the fastest results:** AeroGarden Bounty or Tower Garden HOME (see our [hydroponic tower garden guide](/blog/hydroponic-garden-towers) for the full comparison).

Start with the system that matches where you are right now, not where you hope to be eventually. The skills transfer between systems — learning pH management on a $40 DWC setup gives you exactly the same knowledge you'll use on a $1,000 aeroponic tower later.
